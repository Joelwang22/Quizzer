29  Singer’s argument (complicated version)  Premise 1) If we can prevent something bad from happening, without thereby  sacrificing anything of (comparable) moral importance, we ought to do it.  Premise 2) Suffering and death from extreme poverty are bad.  Premise 3) We can prevent suffering and death from extreme poverty, without thereby sacrificing anything  of (comparable) moral importance.  Conclusion) We ought to prevent suffering and death from extreme poverty.  (That is, it is wrong not to do so; It is our moral obligation).  The drowning child case is supposed to motivate Premise 1 (at least on a common interpretation of Singer)

31  Singer’s argument (simpler version)  Premise 1) We ought to save the drowning child. (That is, it is wrong not to do so; It is our moral obligation).  Premise 2) There are no morally relevant differences between saving the drowning child and helping people  who are dying and suffering from extreme poverty in developing countries.  (In other words, if we ought to save the drowning child, then we ought to help people dying and  suffering from extreme poverty)  Conclusion) Therefore, we ought to help people who are dying and suffering from extreme poverty in  developing countries. (That is, it is wrong not to do so; It is our moral obligation).  (Assuming that this argument is well constructed), if you accept the premises, you must accept the conclusion.  To put it another way, if you want to reject the conclusion, you should reject at least one of the premises.

39  Singer proposes two versions, strong and moderate.  Strong version: “we ought to be preventing as much suffering as we can without sacrificing  something else of   comparable   moral importance.” (p.238)  Moderate version: “we should prevent bad occurrences unless, to do so, we had to sacrifice  something morally significant” (p.241)     we ought to be preventing as much suffering as we can without  sacrificing something else of moral importance.  Even the moderate version is quite demanding: We still ought to donate a substantial amount of money. We’re doing something morally wrong when we buy things like bottled water, new clothes, etc.  “By "without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance" I mean without causing anything else comparably bad to happen, or doing something that is wrong in itself, or failing to promote some moral good, comparable in significance to the bad thing that we can prevent.” (p.231)

--- Page 40 ---
40  <Clarifications about Singer’s view as it is expressed in this paper>  •   Note that even the strong version only concerns reducing pain or suffering, not maximizing total net pleasure or happiness.  •   So, if people in developing countries were already happy and did not suffer, even the strong version wouldn’t require us to help them become happier even if that would maximize total net happiness.  •   Thus, Singer, in the context of this paper, can allow that there are such things as supererogatory actions (e.g., maximizing total net happiness by making already happy people happier).  •   Singer’s claim, thus, is not that there are no such things as supererogatory actions, but that it is not a supererogatory action for us to help people in developing countries (without sacrificing something of (comparable) moral importance). This is morally required; It is wrong not to do so.

42  More objections…  •   You’ve worked hard to get where you are now, so haven’t you earned a right to enjoy it?  “Nobel Prize winning economist and social scientist Herbert Simon estimated that “social capital” is responsible for at least 90 percent of what people earn in wealthy societies…. And most of the poor [in developing countries] do work at least as hard as you.  …having a right to do something doesn’t settle the question of what you should do. If you have a right to do something, I can’t justifiably force you not to do it, but I can still tell you that…you would be wrong to do it. You may have a right to spend your weekend surfing, but it can still be true that you ought to visit your sick mother.  I am not arguing here for higher taxation or any other coercive means of increasing aid.” (The Life You Can Save pp.26-28)

What makes an action morally right or wrong?  We’ve so far discussed one approach, consequentialism.  We’ll now discuss other approaches, which we’ll call non-consequentialism,  and see how they differ from consequentialism  with respect to specific examples, trolley cases.  It is   NOT   the case that whether an act is morally right or wrong depends   ONLY   on consequences.  (Some non-consequentialists think consequences are irrelevant while other non-consequentialists think consequences are one of relevant factors)

--- Page 3 ---
3  Consequentialism  •   Consequentialism: whether an act is morally right or wrong depends   ONLY   on consequences (everything else, such as intention, motive, etc. is irrelevant).  •   There are different kinds of consequentialism, depending on, among other things, what kinds of consequences are relevant.  e.g.) A hypothetical example of consequentialism: a right action is an action that  maximizes the number of cats in the world.  Suppose that there are two actions available to you.  Action A will produce 1 more cat in the world (wrong)  Action B will produce 2 more cats in the world (right)  •   (Classic) utilitarianism is an example of consequentialism.

<Thomson’s argument as to why it is morally wrong to throw the switch  in Bystander’s Two Options (= the original Bystander case)>  Premise 1) It is morally wrong to turn the trolley onto another person in Bystander’s Three Options.  Premise 2) If it is morally wrong to turn the trolley onto another person in Bystander’s Three Options, it is morally wrong to turn the trolley onto one person in Bystander’s Two Options.  Conclusion) Therefore, it is it is morally wrong to turn the trolley onto one person in Bystander’s Two Options.

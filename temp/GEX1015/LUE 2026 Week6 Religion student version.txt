14  •   A   sound   argument is a   valid   argument whose   premises are all true.  Soundness = (1) validity + (2) all premises being true.  Both conditions need to be met for an argument to be sound.  •   A   valid   argument :  Its premises and conclusion are connected in such a way that…  if   the premises are all true, the conclusion must be true as well .  In other words, the conclusion follows from the premises.  The premises, if all true, 100% guarantee the truth of the conclusion.  (It doesn’t matter whether the premises are   actually true .)  How to check whether an argument is valid:  Assume   that all the premises are true and think about whether it is possible for the conclusion to be false.  If yes, invalid. If no, valid.  E.g.) P1. All philosophers are men.  P2. Jungkook is a philosopher.   Sound or unsound?  C. Jungkook is a man.  Unsound

Theists   typically attack P2, by providing explanations as to how evils in the world are justified  (that is, explanations as to what God’s reasons for allowing the evils are or could be).  Note that theists would want to show that   all   evils in the world are justified.  Allowing the evil in question is   necessary   for achieving  a   more important   moral goal (e.g., generating a good  that outweighs the evil, preventing a more serious evil)  (“necessary” here means that even an all - perfect god  cannot achieve that goal without allowing the evil).
